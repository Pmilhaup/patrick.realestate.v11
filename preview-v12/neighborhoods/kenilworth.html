Kenilworth · North Shore

Architectural pedigree at scale.

Kenilworth is the smallest North Shore village by parcel count — and the most architecturally distinguished. Howard Van Doren Shaw and George Washington Maher authored much of the original village, and the storied building stock has been guarded carefully across more than a century. A truly iconic inheritance.

Chapter One Kenilworth Snapshot

A village of pedigree, scale, and scarcity.

Kenilworth's median list price of $2.35M is the highest on the corridor — and the village's $658 price-per-square-foot is the corridor's clearest signal of architectural pedigree commanding a measurable premium. With days-on-market at 26 and sale-to-list ratios of 98.8%, the village trades efficiently when inventory becomes available; supply, however, remains structurally constrained by the small total parcel count. A typical year produces a fraction of the listing volume seen in adjoining villages.

Chapter Two Architecture & Schools

Original vocabulary, anchor schools.

Much of Kenilworth's original village was authored by Howard Van Doren Shaw and George Washington Maher — two of the defining architects of the early-twentieth-century North Shore. The village has guarded that legacy with rare discipline; significant Maher and Shaw commissions remain intact, lovingly restored, and trade with provenance documentation that few other villages can match. Architectural review at the municipal level keeps the rebuild stock consistent with the original vocabulary.

Kenilworth's K–8 students attend the village's single public school, Joseph Sears School (District 38), before entering New Trier Township High School (District 203). The single-school district structure and small graduating classes are a defining feature of village life and a meaningful part of why families remain in Kenilworth for multiple generations.
